The Surveillance Scam Handbook

Criminals use the fear of being watched to steal your money. Learn the psychological triggers and technical traps they use.

Psychological

The 'Pegasus' Sextortion

Scammers claim they have installed spyware on your phone and recorded you via webcam, often using an old leaked password as "proof".

Red Flags:

✖ Urgent threat to release video
✖ Bitcoin payment demand
Technical Phishing

Fake Security Alerts

A pop-up or SMS warns that your security camera has been compromised. It urges you to "Click here to secure your account."

Red Flags:

✖ Links to non-official URLs
✖ Sense of panic
Technical Fraud

The 'IT Admin' Access

A scammer pretends to be from Microsoft/Apple claiming your device is broadcasting errors and asks for remote access to "fix" it.

Red Flags:

✖ Unsolicited help
✖ Asking for AnyDesk access

The "Technical" Surveillance Reality

Man-in-the-Middle (Wi-Fi)

Scammers set up free Wi-Fi in public spaces named "Airport_Guest". They monitor the data passing through to steal credentials entered on non-encrypted sites.

QR Code Spoofing

"Quishing" involves placing a sticker over a real QR code on a parking meter. Scanning it sends you to a fake payment portal that installs tracking cookies.

The Ironclad Defense

Use Hardware Camera Covers

Physical shutters cannot be hacked by software. It removes the psychological threat of "I recorded you."

App-Based MFA

Switch from SMS codes to Authenticator apps. Scammers can intercept SMS but can't easily steal an app token.

The 'Manual Entry' Rule

Never click a security alert link. If your bank warns you, open your browser and type the URL manually.

"Scammers count on your panic. If you feel a rush of fear, that is your signal to stop, breathe, and verify independently."
